    title: "What is gallbladder cancer surgery?"
    body: "Gallbladder cancer surgery is a planned oncological resection that goes beyond a simple cholecystectomy. Depending on the stage, the operation includes removal of the gallbladder along with a wedge of adjacent liver (segments IVb and V), regional lymph node dissection, and sometimes extended liver resection or bile duct resection. The choice of operation depends on tumour stage, whether the cancer was found incidentally during a routine cholecystectomy, and the patient's overall fitness."

  - type: steps
    title: "How a radical cholecystectomy is performed"
    steps:
      - "Open right subcostal or upper midline incision under general anaesthesia"
      - "Staging laparoscopy first, where indicated, to exclude peritoneal spread"
      - "Mobilisation of the liver and inspection of the porta hepatis"
      - "Wedge resection of liver segments IVb and V along with the gallbladder, achieving a clear deep margin"
      - "Systematic lymph node dissection along the hepatoduodenal ligament, common hepatic artery, and portal vein"
      - "Frozen-section examination of the cystic duct margin to ensure clear resection"
      - "Drains are placed; abdomen is closed in layers"
    outro: "More advanced cases may need extended hepatectomy (right or extended right hepatectomy) and bile duct resection with reconstruction. Total operating time varies from 4 to 7 hours."

  - type: faqs
    title: "Frequently asked questions"
    items:
      - question: "What is incidental gallbladder cancer?"
        answer: "It is gallbladder cancer found by the pathologist on routine examination of a gallbladder removed for what was thought to be benign disease. Around 0.5 to 2 percent of routine cholecystectomies turn out to harbour cancer. The next step depends on the cancer's depth — early stage may need no further surgery, deeper invasion warrants re-resection."
      - question: "Can gallbladder cancer be treated laparoscopically?"
        answer: "Open surgery is preferred for established gallbladder cancer because of the risk of port-site recurrence and the need for thorough lymph node dissection and liver-bed clearance. In selected very early cases (T1a) with confirmed clear margins from a previous laparoscopic cholecystectomy, observation alone may be appropriate."
      - question: "What is the survival rate for gallbladder cancer?"
        answer: "It is highly stage dependent. T1a tumours confined to the inner gallbladder lining have excellent outcomes after cholecystectomy alone. T2 and T3 tumours have improved outcomes after radical surgery. Advanced disease (T4 or with distant spread) is generally not curable surgically and is managed with chemotherapy and palliative care."
      - question: "Will I need chemotherapy after surgery?"
        answer: "It depends on the final histopathology. Adjuvant chemotherapy (typically capecitabine) is recommended for stage II and beyond, based on randomised-trial evidence. The decision is made jointly with medical oncology after surgery."
